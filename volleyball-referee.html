The Job

What Does a Volleyball Referee Do?

At its simplest, a volleyball referee enforces the rules of the game. In practice the job is three things at once: a judge of ball contacts and faults, a manager of match flow (substitutions, timeouts, sanctions, the scoresheet), and a communicator who keeps coaches, players, and scorers aligned so the match runs on schedule.

1

Call the Rally

Every point ends in a decision. Ball in or out. Legal set or double contact. Net touch, yes or no. The referee calls it, the whistle sounds, the signal goes up, and the score moves.

2

Manage the Match

Lineups, rotations, substitutions, timeouts, set changes, side switches, libero entries, the scoresheet. Somebody has to track all of it. That somebody is usually the officiating crew.

3

Keep It Moving

A well-officiated match has tempo. Coaches respect the crew. Sanctions are used sparingly but firmly. The best officials are invisible in all the right ways.

The Two Referees

R1 and R2. Different Jobs. One Crew.

Indoor volleyball uses two referees. They share the workload by splitting the field of view and the administrative duties. Beach volleyball uses the same two-referee model.

R1, the First Referee

The final authority on the rally. Stands on a raised platform above one end of the net so they see the ball cross the plane of the net clearly. Calls ball contacts, antenna faults, and any decision that is disputed. Their whistle starts and ends every rally.

Because the R1 is elevated, they get the best view of net touches, ball trajectory at the antennas, and in/out calls near the sideline on their side. Their job is to see the rally and judge it.

R2, the Second Referee

Works at floor level on the opposite side of the net from the R1. Manages the administrative side of the match: substitutions, timeouts, the lineup, libero entries, and communication with the scorekeeper.

The R2 also watches for the things the R1 cannot see from the stand: foot faults under the net, back-row attack violations, and player positions at the moment of service. Together the two referees cover the whole match.

Rules in Practice

The Rules You Will Actually Use.

There are hundreds of rules in the FIVB and USAV rulebooks. A handful come up every match. Master these and you will be ready for 95 percent of what happens on the court.

Scoring and Set Structure

Matches are best of five sets. The first four are played to 25 points with a two-point margin. The fifth set is played to 15 with the same two-point margin. See our volleyball scoring rules guide for the full breakdown.

Rotations

Players rotate clockwise when their team wins a rally the opponent was serving. The server is always in position 1. Front row versus back row determines who can attack at the net. Our rotation rules page walks through the whole system with diagrams.

Substitutions

Each set allows a limited number of substitutions. A starter can leave and re-enter once in the same position they started. Libero entries and exits are tracked separately and do not count against the team's regular sub total. See substitution rules for the full mechanics.

Libero

The libero wears a contrasting jersey, plays only in the back row, and replaces other back-row players without counting as a substitution. Liberos cannot complete an attack from in front of the 3-meter line. Tracking libero entries is one of the easier places to lose the scoresheet if you are not paying attention.

Timeouts

Each team gets two timeouts per set. In some levels there are also technical timeouts at preset scores. The R2 tracks the timeouts and starts the clock; the scorer records them.

Sanctions

Unsporting conduct, delays, and misconduct escalate through verbal warning, yellow card, red card, and ejection. See the sanction card section of your governing body's rulebook for the progression; enforce it consistently and the match stays under control.

Hand Signals

The Signals Every Referee Must Know.

Hand signals are how a referee communicates the call to the players, coaches, scorer, and spectators. There are roughly twenty standard FIVB signals you will use regularly. Here is a preview of the ones you will signal most often.

Point to the Serving Team

Extend your arm toward the side that won the rally, showing who serves next. This is the most common signal you will give.

Ball In

Point toward the floor inside the court. A single definitive gesture. Used after any close in-or-out decision on the endline or sideline.

Ball Out

Raise both forearms vertically with palms facing the body. Indicates the ball landed outside the court boundaries or touched the antenna.

Double Contact

Raise two fingers with your palm up. Called on a second team contact that was handled twice by the same player, most commonly on a set.

Four Hits

Raise four fingers. The team had more than three contacts before sending the ball back over the net.

Net Touch

Touch the top of the net on the side of the player who made contact. This is usually a clean call at the R1 level because you are watching the net plane.

Getting Started

How to Become a Volleyball Referee.

There is no mystery to getting certified. The path is the same for club, high school, and college officials at the entry level. Expect a few weeks of study, a written exam, and a short ride-along period with a mentor.

1

Register With a Governing Body

In the United States, that is USA Volleyball for club and PAVO or the NFHS for high school. Each has an online registration portal and an annual fee.

2

Complete the Rules Clinic

A required online course covering the current rulebook, mechanics, and case plays. Allow eight to twelve hours.

3

Pass the Written Exam

Multiple choice test on the rulebook. Most bodies require 80 percent to pass. Retakes are allowed.

4

Attend a Clinic or Camp

An in-person session where you practice mechanics, work mock matches, and ask questions. Usually a full Saturday.

5

Work With a Mentor

Your first few matches are paired with a veteran referee who evaluates your mechanics and gives feedback.

6

Start Officiating

Once certified, you can accept assignments through your local officiating coordinator. Club matches, high school, tournaments.

Pay and Hours

How Much Do Volleyball Referees Make?

Officiating volleyball is part-time work for most people. Rates vary by level and region. Expect these ranges for a single match in the United States.

Club Matches

$20 to $40 per match at the entry level. Youth club tournaments often pay a flat day rate instead of per-match.

High School

$55 to $110 per match for varsity. Junior varsity runs lower. Travel stipends vary by conference.

College

$150 to $400+ per match depending on division. NCAA Division I is the top end; junior colleges and Division III pay less but are more accessible.

Tournament Day Rates

$150 to $300 for a full Saturday at a regional or national qualifier. Long days but predictable pay.

Equipment You Actually Need.

The official kit is small. A whistle, a uniform, and something to track the match on. Everything else is optional.

Whistle

A Fox 40 or Acme Thunderer is standard. Pealess whistles work in humidity and on dusty courts. Carry a backup on a lanyard around your neck.

Uniform

Most governing bodies require a white polo and dark slacks or skirt. Court shoes with non-marking soles. Some associations require an approved patch on the shirt.

Rulebook or Rules App

A physical rulebook in your bag for reference, or a digital rules app on your phone. Use whichever you can find fast when a coach asks a question between sets.

Sanction Cards

A yellow card and a red card. Tucked into a shirt pocket. You will use them rarely and nobody cares what brand.

Stopwatch

A simple digital watch or dedicated stopwatch for timeout and interval timing. Phones work too.

Scoring Tool

A paper lineup card and scoresheet, or a digital scoring app like volleyref.app that handles the scoresheet, substitution counts, libero tracking, and set intervals automatically. The digital path is faster and eliminates a huge category of paperwork errors.

Learn the Hard Way

The Most Common Mistakes New Referees Make.

Every new official makes these. Knowing them in advance does not prevent them entirely but it shortens the learning curve.

Calling Double Contacts Too Tight.

The FIVB and USAV rules allow much more than most referees think on the first team hit. A tight double-contact whistle kills rallies and frustrates coaches. When in doubt, let it play.

Losing the Serving Order.

After a substitution or a set restart, it is easy to confuse who should serve next. Track serving order on paper or in an app that does it automatically.

Missing Net Touches on Off-Ball Players.

The hitter blocks at the net; the setter brushes the net on the follow-through; the referee is watching the ball. Train your eyes to scan the whole net plane after contact.

Letting the Tempo Slow Down.

A good match has rhythm. If players start loitering between points or coaches drag out timeouts, pick up your whistle cadence and keep the match moving.

Forgetting to Track Libero Entries.

Libero replacements are easy to miss because they do not use the substitution zone. Write down or dispatch every entry; the scoresheet will thank you.

Being Too Slow on Sanctions.

The first verbal warning should come early. Delaying sets the tone that behavior will go unchecked, and the next escalation always hurts more than the first one would have.
