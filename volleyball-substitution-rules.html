Volleyball Substitution Rules Explained.

Substitution rules are where most scorekeepers get tripped up. Limits vary by ruleset, positional restrictions apply, and libero replacements follow completely different rules.

FIVB: 6 Subs USAV: 18 Subs NFHS: 18 Subs NCAA: 15 Subs
Per-Set Limits Varies by ruleset
Positional Restriction Player pairs
Exceptional Subs Injury/DQ rules
Libero = Different Not a sub
The Basics

How Substitutions Work.

Before diving into ruleset-specific limits, here is what every substitution has in common.

1

Replace One Player

A substitution replaces one player on the court with a player on the bench. One in, one out.

2

Dead Ball Only

Can only happen during a dead ball, between rallies. Never during live play.

3

Same Position

The substitute enters in the same position as the player leaving. No position changes happen.

4

Counts Against the Limit

Each sub counts against the per-set limit. Once you hit zero remaining, no more subs for that set.

Know Your Ruleset

Substitution Limits by Ruleset.

The number of subs allowed per set varies significantly. Know which ruleset youre using before the match starts.

FIVB: 6 Subs Per Set

Strict limit. Once used, no more for the set. International rules keep substitutions tight.

USAV: 18 Subs Per Set

More flexible. Allows more lineup changes throughout the set. Common in club volleyball.

NFHS: 18 Subs Per Set

Same as USAV for most practical purposes. High school volleyball uses the same generous limit.

NCAA: 15 Subs Per Set

Between FIVB and USAV. College volleyball splits the difference on substitution limits.

Positional Restrictions

Player Pairs Are Locked.

Substitutions create pairings that last the entire set. This is the rule most people get wrong.

1

Player A Subs Out for Player B

When Player A leaves the court and Player B enters, those two players are linked for the rest of the set.

2

Player B Can Only Sub Back for Player A

Player B cannot sub out for anyone else. Only Player A can replace Player B on the court.

3

Two Players Per Position

This means each position has at most 2 players who can fill it during a set. No exceptions.

4

Resets Each Set

These paired relationships reset at the start of each new set. New set, clean slate.

Edge Cases

Exceptional Substitutions.

Injury During Play.

If a player is injured and cannot continue, an exceptional sub may be allowed outside normal rules. The specifics depend on the ruleset.

Disqualification.

When a player gets a red+yellow card (DQ), they must be replaced. The rules for who can replace them differ between FIVB and USAV.

Expulsion vs DQ.

An expelled player (yellow+red) is out for the current set but can return next set. A disqualified player is out for the match.

Different Rules Entirely

Libero Replacements Are Not Substitutions.

This is the single biggest source of confusion. The libero follows a completely separate set of rules.

Different Jersey, Different Rules

The libero wears a different jersey color and follows separate rules. Libero replacements do not count against the substitution limit.

Back Row Only

The libero can only replace back-row players. They never play in the front row.

Wait One Rally

After coming off the court, the libero must wait one completed rally before going back on. Miss this and its a violation.

Watch Out

Common Substitution Mistakes.

Losing Count.

Was that the 5th or 6th sub? Three subs came in at once. The count gets away from you fast.

Breaking Positional Pairs.

Player A subbed out for Player B earlier. Now you try to sub Player C in for Player A. Illegal. The positional pairing is locked.

Confusing Libero with Subs.

A libero replacement is not a substitution. If you count it as one, youll think the team has fewer subs remaining than they actually do.
